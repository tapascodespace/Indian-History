The Later Deccan — Hoysaḷa, Kākatīya & Yādava

The kingdoms of the Deccan, c. 1100 – c. 1320 CE — the last Hindu powers before the Delhi Sultanate’s southern campaigns
I

The Later Deccan

As the Kalyāṇī Chālukya empire fragmented in the twelfth century, three successor dynasties rose to dominate the Deccan: the Hoysaḷas of Dvārasamudra (modern Halebidu), the Kākatīyas of Warangal, and the Yādavas (Seunas) of Devagiri. Each produced remarkable achievements in art and architecture — the Hoysaḷa temples at Belur and Halebidu, the Kākatīya Thousand Pillar Temple at Warangal, and the Yādava patronage of Marāṭhī literature.

All three were ultimately destroyed by Ala-ud-din Khilji’s armies in the early fourteenth century, when the Delhi Sultanate extended its power into the deep south for the first time. Their fall left a political vacuum that would be filled by the Vijayanagara and Bahmani kingdoms.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Hoysaḷa

Dvārasamudra · c. 1026–1343 CE

The Hoysaḷa temples at Belur, Halebidu, and Somnathpur are among the supreme achievements of Indian architecture — soapstone carved with jewel-like precision into intricate friezes of gods, dancers, and animals. Vishnuvardhana (r. 1108–1152) established independence from the Chālukyas; Ballāla III was the last significant ruler before the Delhi Sultanate's campaigns ended Hoysaḷa power.

Kākatīya

Warangal · c. 1083–1323 CE

The Kākatīyas built the great fortress of Warangal with its concentric walls and famous ornamental gateways. Under Ganapatideva and his daughter Rudrāmba (one of the few reigning queens of medieval India, praised by Marco Polo), the kingdom flourished. Destroyed by the Delhi Sultanate in 1323 CE.

Yādava (Seuna)

Devagiri · c. 850–1317 CE

The Yādavas of Devagiri (modern Daulatabad) controlled Maharashtra. Under their patronage, Marāṭhī emerged as a literary language — the saint-poet tradition (Dnāndev, Nāmdev) that defines Marathi culture began in this era. Ala-ud-din Khilji raided Devagiri in 1296 and conquered it outright in 1317.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

Delhi Sultanate’s Southern Campaigns

Ala-ud-din Khilji (r. 1296–1316) launched devastating raids into the Deccan, beginning with Devagiri (1296) and culminating in the conquest of all three successor states by his general Malik Kafur. The Tughluqs completed the absorption by the 1320s–1340s.

Indian Ocean Trade

The Later Deccan kingdoms maintained active maritime trade networks. Kākatīya-era ports on the east coast traded with Southeast Asia and China. Horse imports from Arabia and Persia were a crucial military resource for all Deccan kingdoms.

Mongol Pressure on Delhi

The Mongol invasions of the early thirteenth century pressured the Delhi Sultanate from the northwest, making its southern campaigns partly an attempt to secure resources and territory away from the Mongol frontier.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

Hoysaḷa Temple Art

The Hoysaḷa temples are carved from soapstone (chloritic schist) — a soft stone that hardens on exposure — allowing extraordinary sculptural detail. Every surface is covered with horizontal friezes: elephants, horsemen, scrollwork, mythological narratives, and standing deities. Belur’s Chennakesava and Halebidu’s Hoysalesvara are masterpieces of world architecture.

Marāṭhī & Telugu Literature

The Yādava court saw the emergence of Marāṭhī as a literary language. Mukundāj's Vivekasindhu and the early Vārkari saints laid the foundations of Marathi literature. In Kākatīya Andhra, Telugu literature flowered under Tikkana (who completed the Telugu Mahābhārata).

Fort Architecture

The Warangal fort and Devagiri fort represent the mature phase of Deccan military architecture — concentric walls, rock-cut moats, and elaborate gateways. Devagiri’s hilltop fortress would later be renamed Daulatabad by the Tughluqs.

A Note on Method

This plate covers the period c. 1100–1320 CE, when three independent Deccan kingdoms coexisted before their destruction by the Delhi Sultanate. The map shows approximate territories at their mature extent (c. 1200 CE), before the Khilji invasions.

The transition from these kingdoms to the Vijayanagara and Bahmani states (see next plates) represents one of the most dramatic political ruptures in Indian history.
