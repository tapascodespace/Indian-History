The Delhi Sultanate — Five Dynasties

The kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 1206 – c. 1526 CE — from the Slave Dynasty to the Lodīs, the Tughluq maximum and Timur’s sack
I

The Delhi Sultanate

For over three centuries (1206–1526 CE), the Delhi Sultanate — ruling from the Qutb Minar-crowned capital — dominated northern India and, at its peak under the Tughluqs, briefly controlled nearly the entire subcontinent. Five dynasties succeeded each other: the Mamluks (Slave dynasty), Khiljīs, Tughluqs, Sayyids, and Lodīs.

The Sultanate brought Persian administrative models, Turkic military organisation, and Islamic culture to the subcontinent. Its greatest architectural legacy — the Qutb Minar, Alai Darwaza, Tughluqabad, and the Lodī Gardens — transformed Delhi into one of the great cities of the medieval Islamic world.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Mamluk (Slave) Dynasty

Delhi · c. 1206–1290 CE

Founded by Qutb-ud-din Aibak, consolidated by Iltutmish, and defended against the Mongols by Balban. The Qutb Minar — India’s tallest stone tower — was begun by Aibak and completed by Iltutmish. Razia Sultana (r. 1236–1240) was one of the few female rulers in the medieval Islamic world.

Khiljī Dynasty

Delhi · c. 1290–1320 CE

Ala-ud-din Khiljī (r. 1296–1316) was the most powerful Khilji sultan. He repulsed multiple Mongol invasions, conquered the Deccan kingdoms through his general Malik Kafur, and implemented sweeping economic reforms and price controls. His Alai Darwaza gateway is a masterpiece of Indo-Islamic architecture.

Tughluq Dynasty

Delhi · c. 1320–1414 CE

Muhammad bin Tughluq (r. 1325–1351) pushed the Sultanate to its maximum extent — from Peshawar to Madurai — but his ambitious and sometimes erratic policies (shifting the capital to Daulatabad, issuing token currency) weakened the state. Firuz Shah Tughluq (r. 1351–1388) consolidated but could not prevent decline. Timur’s sack of Delhi (1398 CE) devastated the city.

Lodī Dynasty

Delhi · c. 1451–1526 CE

The first Afghan (Pashtun) dynasty to rule Delhi. Ibrahim Lodī was the last Sultan — defeated and killed by Babur at the First Battle of Panipat (1526 CE), which founded the Mughal Empire.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

Mongol Invasions

The Delhi Sultanate faced repeated Mongol incursions from the 1220s through the early 1300s. Balban and Ala-ud-din Khiljī repulsed major invasions that might otherwise have destroyed the Sultanate. India was one of very few regions to successfully resist the Mongol expansion.

Timur’s Invasion (1398 CE)

Timur (Tamerlane) sacked Delhi in December 1398, carrying away immense plunder and leaving the city in ruins. The Tughluq dynasty never recovered; the subsequent Sayyid and Lodī dynasties ruled a much-diminished state.

Rise of Regional Sultanates

As Tughluq power collapsed after 1347, independent sultanates emerged across India: Bahmani (Deccan), Bengal, Jaunpur, Malwa, Gujarat, Kashmir. These regional states — each with distinctive architecture and culture — defined the political landscape of fifteenth-century India.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

Indo-Islamic Architecture

The Delhi Sultanate created a new architectural tradition: Indo-Islamic architecture. The Qutb Minar, Alai Darwaza, Tughluqabad fortress, Hauz Khas complex, and the Lodī Gardens tombs represent successive phases of this style — from the re-use of Hindu temple columns to fully developed Sultanate forms.

Persian & Urdu Literature

The Sultanate court was a centre of Persian literary culture. Amir Khusrau (1253–1325) — poet, musician, and Sufi mystic — is regarded as the father of Urdu literature and Hindustani classical music. The Sultanate period saw the emergence of Urdu as a literary language from the interaction of Persian, Arabic, and local vernaculars.

Sufi Orders

The Chishti, Suhrawardi, and Naqshbandi Sufi orders spread across India during the Sultanate period. Saints like Nizām-ud-dīn Auliyā and Bu Alī Shah Qalandar attracted devotees across religious lines, creating a syncretic spiritual culture that profoundly shaped Indian civilisation.

VI

A Note on Method

The Delhi Sultanate lasted 320 years and five dynasties. This map shows the empire at its maximum extent under Muhammad bin Tughluq (c. 1335 CE), when the Sultanate briefly controlled nearly the entire subcontinent. In practice, this maximum was short-lived — the Deccan and the south broke away within a decade.

The later Sultanate (Sayyid and Lodī periods) controlled only a fraction of this territory — essentially the Delhi-Agra corridor and parts of the Punjab.
