The Empire of Victory — Vijayanagara

The kingdoms of the Deccan, c. 1336 – c. 1646 CE — the last great Hindu empire of southern India and the rival Bahmani/Deccan Sultanates
I

The Empire of Victory

Vijayanagara — the “City of Victory” — was the last great Hindu empire of southern India, founded in 1336 CE amid the ruins of the Delhi Sultanate’s collapsed southern provinces. For over three centuries, the empire served as a bulwark against Sultanate expansion, reaching its zenith under Krishnadevaraya (r. 1509–1529), one of the most celebrated rulers in Indian history.

The capital city of Vijayanagara (modern Hampi) was, at its height, one of the largest and wealthiest cities in the world — described in wonder by Portuguese, Persian, and Italian travellers. The empire’s rival was the Bahmani Sultanate (and its successor Deccan Sultanates), with the Tungabhadra river as the traditional frontier. The catastrophic Battle of Talikota (1565 CE) destroyed the capital but the empire lingered in diminished form until the mid-seventeenth century.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Vijayanagara Empire

Hampi · c. 1336–1646 CE

Founded by Harihara I and Bukka Rāya I under the guidance of the sage Vidyāraṇya. The empire reached its peak under the Tuluva dynasty’s Krishnadevaraya (r. 1509–1529), who was a poet, patron of arts, and military genius. The capital city of Hampi — with its towering gopuras, bazaars, and royal enclosures — astounded every traveller who saw it. The catastrophic Battle of Talikota (1565 CE) destroyed the city, though the empire survived in diminished form at Penukonda and Chandragiri until 1646.

Bahmani Sultanate

Gulbarga / Bidar · c. 1347–1527 CE

Founded by Ala-ud-din Hasan Bahman Shah, the Bahmani Sultanate was the principal rival of Vijayanagara. After 1490, it fragmented into five successor states: Bijapur, Golconda, Ahmadnagar, Berar, and Bidar. It was an alliance of these “Deccan Sultanates” that destroyed Vijayanagara at Talikota.

Portuguese Goa

Goa · c. 1510+

The Portuguese conquest of Goa (1510 CE) introduced European colonial power to the Indian Ocean. Vijayanagara and the Portuguese maintained a complex relationship — trading partners and sometimes military allies against the Deccan Sultanates.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

Portuguese & Indian Ocean Trade

The Portuguese arrival in the Indian Ocean (1498 CE) transformed the maritime landscape. Goa, conquered in 1510, became the capital of the Portuguese Estado da Índia. Vijayanagara traded horses with the Portuguese and sometimes allied with them against the Sultanates.

The Deccan Sultanates

After the Bahmani kingdom fragmented (c. 1490), the five successor sultanates — Bijapur, Golconda, Ahmadnagar, Berar, Bidar — fought among themselves and against Vijayanagara. Their alliance at Talikota (1565) destroyed the Vijayanagara capital but did not create a unified successor state.

Mughal Expansion

By the late sixteenth century, the Mughal Empire was expanding southward into the Deccan. Akbar conquered Berar and parts of Ahmadnagar; his successors would eventually absorb all the Deccan Sultanates by 1687.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

Hampi — The City of Victory

The capital city of Hampi (a UNESCO World Heritage Site) sprawled across 25 square kilometres among spectacular boulder-strewn granite hills. Its Vitthala Temple (with its iconic stone chariot and musical pillars), Hazara Rāma Temple, and royal enclosures are among the finest monuments of medieval India.

Telugu & Kannada Literature

Krishnadevaraya’s court was a golden age of Telugu literature — the king himself composed Āmuktamālyadā, a Telugu masterpiece. The Aṣṭadiggajas (Eight Elephants of poetry) flourished at his court. Kannada literature also received significant patronage.

Music & Dance

The Vijayanagara period consolidated the Carnatic music tradition and classical Bharatanatyam dance. The temple sculptures at Hampi depict dancers and musicians in extraordinary detail, providing crucial evidence for the reconstruction of medieval performance traditions.

VI

A Note on Method

The Vijayanagara empire lasted 310 years across four dynasties (Sangama, Saluva, Tuluva, Aravidu). This map shows the empire at approximately its peak under Krishnadevaraya (c. 1520 CE), when it controlled most of peninsular India south of the Tungabhadra.

After the Battle of Talikota (1565 CE), the empire retreated southward; the Aravidu dynasty ruled from Penukonda and Chandragiri until the mid-seventeenth century.
