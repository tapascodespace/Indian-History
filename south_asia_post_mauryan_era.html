After the Mauryas — Shungas, Greeks & Sakas

The kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 185 BCE – c. 50 CE — fragmentation after Mauryan collapse and Hellenistic contact
I

After the Mauryas

When the last Mauryan emperor Brihadratha was assassinated by his general Puṣyamitra Śuṅga in 185 BCE, the greatest empire the subcontinent had known fractured into a mosaic of successor states. The Śuṅga dynasty held the Gangetic heartland from Pāṭaliputra, but the northwest was lost — first to the Indo-Greek kings of Bactria, who brought Hellenistic art and coinage deep into the Punjab and beyond, and then to waves of Indo-Scythian (Saka) and Indo-Parthian invaders from Central Asia.

This was an age of extraordinary cultural mixing: Greek-legend coins circulated alongside Brāhmī-inscribed punch-marks; the philosopher-king Menander I (Milinda) debated Buddhist monks in Sagala; and the warrior-king Khāravela of Kaliṅga left an epic rock inscription at Hāthigumphā boasting of campaigns from Magadha to the Tamil south.

The map below shows this fragmented world — the Śuṅga rump in the east, the Indo-Greek kingdoms in the northwest, the rising Sātavāhanas in the Deccan, and the Tamil Sangam kingdoms in the far south.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Śuṅga Empire

Pāṭaliputra · c. 185–73 BCE

Founded by Puṣyamitra Śuṅga, a Brahmin general who overthrew the last Maurya. The Śuṅgas patronised Brahmanical religion — Puṣyamitra performed the Vedic aśvamedha (horse sacrifice) — but Buddhist art also continued to flourish under their rule, as seen in the railing sculptures at Bhārhut and the gateways at Sāñchī. They were succeeded by the short-lived Kaṇva dynasty.

Indo-Greek Kingdoms

Sagala (Sialkot) · c. 180 BCE–10 CE

The Indo-Greeks were the easternmost outpost of the Hellenistic world. Menander I (Milinda), the greatest Indo-Greek king, is immortalised in the Buddhist text Milindapañha (Questions of Milinda). Indo-Greek coins are among the most beautiful of the ancient world, and their bilingual legends (Greek and Kharoṣṭhī) reflect a genuinely multicultural state.

Indo-Scythians (Sakas)

Northwest India · c. 110 BCE–400 CE

Central Asian nomads who displaced the Indo-Greeks from parts of Gandhāra and expanded into Gujarat and western India. Their most famous ruling line became the Western Kshatrapas of Ujjain, who would dominate western India for centuries and clash repeatedly with the Sātavāhanas.

Kaliṅga under Khāravela

Hāthigumphā · c. 170–150 BCE

The warrior-king Khāravela of the Mahameghavāhana dynasty left the famous Hāthigumphā inscription at Udayagiri, recording campaigns against the Śuṅgas, the Sātavāhanas, and even the Tamil kingdoms. A Jain devotee, he also patronised cave excavation. Kaliṅga’s power faded after his reign.

Early Sātavāhana

Pratiṣṭhāna · c. 100 BCE–220 CE

The Sātavāhanas were rising in the Deccan during this period, gradually building the power base that would make them the dominant Deccan dynasty in the centuries that followed. Their early history is obscure, but by the first century CE they controlled a significant territory across the central Deccan.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

Hellenistic World & Bactria

The Indo-Greek kingdoms maintained connections with the wider Hellenistic world through Bactria. Greek philosophical, artistic, and monetary traditions blended with Indian ones to create a distinctive Indo-Greek cultural synthesis visible in Gandhāra art and bilingual coinage.

Central Asian Migrations

The displacement of the Yuezhi by the Xiongnu set off a chain of migrations that brought the Sakas, Pahlavas (Indo-Parthians), and eventually the Kuṣāṇas into the subcontinent. These waves of migration fundamentally reshaped the political and cultural landscape of northern India.

Roman Trade Begins

By the late first century BCE, direct Roman trade with India via the Red Sea route was beginning. The monsoon-driven sea route, described later in the Periplus, would make the western Indian ports among the busiest in the ancient world.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

Buddhist Art Flourishes

Despite the Śuṅgas' Brahmanical orientation, Buddhist art reached new heights. The gateways (toraṇas) of Sāñchī and the reliefs of Bhārhut are masterpieces of narrative sculpture. In Gandhāra, Hellenistic influence was producing the first experiments that would later yield the Graeco-Buddhist art tradition.

Grammar & Philosophy

Patañjali’s Mahābhāṣya, the great commentary on Pāṇini’s grammar, was composed in this period — a landmark of Sanskrit linguistic thought. Buddhist philosophical schools were also multiplying, and the debates between Buddhist, Jain, and Brahmanical thinkers created one of the richest intellectual environments in the ancient world.

Coinage Revolution

The Indo-Greeks introduced the most sophisticated coinage India had seen — die-struck, bilingual, with realistic royal portraits. This standard influenced all subsequent Indian coinage, from the Saka and Kuṣāṇa series to the Gupta gold coins.

VI

A Note on Method

This plate covers over two centuries of rapid political change — the rise and fall of the Śuṅgas, the expansion and fragmentation of the Indo-Greeks, and the gradual infiltration of the Sakas. The map shows approximate territories at the mid-point of the period (c. 100–50 BCE), when the Śuṅga/Kaṇva, Indo-Greek, and early Sātavāhana powers overlapped.

All boundaries are interpretive. This was an era of fluid, overlapping claims rather than fixed borders; every coloured area should be read as a sphere of influence.

Sources & Further Reading

Primary Sources

  • Hāthigumphā inscription of Khāravela — the major epigraphic source for post-Mauryan eastern India.
  • Indo-Greek bilingual coin series — Greek and Kharoṣṭhī legends.
  • Milindapañha (Questions of Milinda) — Buddhist dialogue with king Menander I.
  • Bhārhut and Sāñchī inscriptions — donor records and Buddhist narratives.
