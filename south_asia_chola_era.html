I

The Imperial Cholas

The Chola dynasty of Thanjavur achieved something unprecedented in Indian history: a naval empire that projected military and commercial power across the Indian Ocean. Under Rājarāja I (r. 985–1014) and his son Rājendra I (r. 1014–1044), the Cholas conquered Sri Lanka, raided the Śrīvijaya maritime kingdom in Southeast Asia, and marched a victorious army to the banks of the Ganges.

The Bṛhadīśvara temple at Thanjavur — commissioned by Rājarāja I — is the supreme monument of Chola art and one of the greatest temples in India. The Chola bronze sculptures, especially the iconic Naṭarāja (Shiva as lord of dance), are among the finest metal artworks ever produced.

The map below shows the Chola empire at its height — the vast domain stretching from the Tungabhadra to Sri Lanka, with naval reach extending to Southeast Asia — flanked by the Kalyāṇī Chālukyas to the north and the Pāṇḍya kingdom to the south.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Chola Empire

Thanjavur · c. 985–1150 CE

Rājarāja I (r. 985–1014) built the Bṛhadīśvara temple at Thanjavur, conquered northern Sri Lanka, and established Chola naval supremacy. His son Rājendra I (r. 1014–1044) surpassed him — marching to the Ganges, raiding Śrīvijaya in Southeast Asia, and building a new capital at Gaṅgaikoṇḍachoḻapuram. The Chola administrative system — with its elaborate village self-governance recorded in detailed temple inscriptions — was among the most sophisticated in pre-modern Asia.

Kalyāṇī Chālukya

Kalyāṇī · c. 973–1189 CE

The Western Chālukyas of Kalyāṇī were the Cholas' principal rival in the Deccan. A long series of Chola–Chālukya wars defined the boundary between the two empires along the Tungabhadra river. Under Vikramāditya VI, the Chālukyas achieved stability, but their feudatories — Hoysaḷa, Kākatīya — were growing restive.

Chola Naval Power

Indian Ocean · c. 1000–1070 CE

The Chola navy was the most powerful in the Indian Ocean. Rājendra I's Śrīvijaya expedition (c. 1025 CE) struck at the maritime kingdom controlling the Strait of Malacca, ensuring the free flow of trade between India and China. Chola trading guilds — the Ayyāvole 500 and the Maṇigrāmam — operated across Southeast Asia.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

Southeast Asia & Śrīvijaya

Rājendra I's naval raid on Śrīvijaya (c. 1025 CE) was a watershed in Indian Ocean history. It broke Śrīvijaya's monopoly on the Strait of Malacca and opened the sea route between India and Song China. Chola influence is visible in the temple architecture and epigraphy of Cambodia, Java, and Sumatra.

Song China

The Cholas maintained diplomatic relations with Song dynasty China. Chinese records describe Chola embassies, and the flourishing maritime trade between Quanzhou and the Tamil ports brought Chinese ceramics, silk, and copper to South India in exchange for gems, spices, and textiles.

Sri Lanka

Rājarāja I conquered northern Sri Lanka, and for nearly eight decades the Cholas ruled the island directly. This period saw the destruction of Anurādhapura as the Sinhalese capital and the eventual shift to Polonnaruwa. Chola rule in Sri Lanka ended with the Sinhalese revival under Vijayabāhu I (c. 1070 CE).

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

The Bṛhadīśvara Temple

The Bṛhadīśvara temple at Thanjavur — built by Rājarāja I — is one of the greatest works of architecture in the world. Its vimāna (tower) rises to 66 metres, capped by a 80-ton granite capstone. The temple is a compendium of Chola art: vast wall paintings, narrative reliefs, inscriptions listing the temple's vast endowments. It is a UNESCO World Heritage Site.

Chola Bronzes

The Chola bronze sculptures are the supreme achievement of Indian metalwork. The iconic Naṭarāja — Shiva as cosmic dancer — created in this period has become one of the most recognised images in world art. The lost-wax (cire perdue) technique used by Chola metalworkers produced sculptures of extraordinary grace and technical perfection.

Tamil Literature & Inscriptions

The Chola period saw the compilation of the Tirumuṟai (Śaiva devotional canon) and continued the Āḻvār Vaiṣṇava tradition. Chola temple inscriptions — numbering in the tens of thousands — are among the most detailed administrative records of the pre-modern world, documenting land grants, village governance, and economic activity.

VI

A Note on Method

This plate covers the peak of Chola imperial power (c. 985–1150 CE). The map shows the empire at approximately its maximum extent under Rājendra I (c. 1025 CE), when Chola power reached from the Tungabhadra to Sri Lanka and the Chola navy had just conducted the Śrīvijaya campaign.

As with all plates in this series, boundaries are interpretive. The Chola–Chālukya frontier along the Tungabhadra shifted with each campaign. Sri Lanka was directly governed; Southeast Asian contacts were primarily military and commercial rather than territorial.
