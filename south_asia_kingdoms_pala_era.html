A Historical Cartography

South Asia in the Age of the Pālas

The major kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 750 – c. 1150 CE — a composite view across the four centuries of Pāla rule in Bengal

I

The Pāla Age

For four centuries, from the mid-eighth to the mid-twelfth, the Pāla dynasty of Bengal and Bihar stood among the great powers of the Indian subcontinent. The age opens around 750 CE, when the nobles of a leaderless Gauḍa are said to have elected Gopāla I as king — a rare moment of consensual kingship in early medieval India — and it closes around 1150–1161, as the dynasty dissolved into the rising Sena power. Between those dates the Pālas were, in turn, an imperial force contending for the throne of Kanauj, the most reliable patrons Buddhism would ever again find in its Indian homeland, and a settled regional monarchy bound to its neighbours by war and by marriage.

This page treats the Pāla centuries as a single historical unit because they share one defining condition: a subcontinent of no paramount power. No single empire ruled the whole; instead, a balance of regional kingdoms — Pratihāra, Rāṣṭrakūṭa and Pāla in the famous tripartite struggle for the north, the Cholas in the deep south, a constellation of Rajput and Deccan houses between — rose and fell against one another. The map below shows those kingdoms together, not as they stood in any one year, but as a composite portrait of the political world the Pālas inhabited.

Reading the Map

A full key sits in the legend cartouche at the lower-left of the map itself. Note the two weights given to territory: a solid fill marks a dynasty’s settled core, while diagonal hatching marks its wider sphere of influence — the contested reach claimed at the height of its power. The three hatched fields of the Pālas, Gurjara-Pratihāras and Rāṣṭrakūṭas deliberately overlap on the Gangetic plain: that overlap is the tripartite struggle for Kanauj.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

The side panel lists every power on the map with its dates and colour. The cards below add the interpretation a historian would want — who each dynasty was, why it mattered, and how it stood in relation to the Pālas of Bengal.

Pāla Empire

Bengal & Bihar · c. 750–1161

Founded by the elected king Gopāla I and raised to imperial rank by Dharmapāla and Devapāla, who briefly held sway over Kanauj and the Gangetic plain. After a long ninth-century decline the dynasty was revived by Mahīpāla I, before yielding to the Senas. The Pālas were the last great royal patrons of Buddhism in India.

Gurjara-Pratihāra

Kanauj · c. 730–1036

The Pālas’ chief northern rival and the third corner of the tripartite struggle. From their capital at Kanauj the Pratihāras shielded the plains against Arab expansion from Sindh, until succession disputes and the sack of Kanauj by Maḥmūd of Ghazni broke their power.

Rāṣṭrakūṭa

Mānyakheṭa · c. 753–982

The dominant Deccan empire, repeatedly intervening in the north and twice sacking Kanauj. They were Pāla allies and in-laws: marriage ties linked the houses, and Rāṣṭrakūṭa pressure on the Pratihāras indirectly aided Bengal.

Western & Eastern Chālukya

Kalyāṇī & Veṅgī · c. 973–1189

The Western Chālukyas of Kalyāṇī overthrew the Rāṣṭrakūṭas and inherited the Deccan, waging a long contest with the Cholas. Their Eastern cousins ruled Veṅgī in coastal Andhra, a frequent prize between Deccan and Tamil powers.

Kalacuri of Chedi

Tripurī · c. 850–1211

A central-Indian Haihaya house and a marital ally of the Pālas — Kalacuri princesses Lajjā and Yauvanaśrī married into the Bengal dynasty. Strategically placed, Chedi was a buffer and bridge between the northern and Deccan powers.

Chola Empire

Thañjāvūr · c. 848–1279

The supreme power of the south under Rājarāja I and Rājendra I, with a navy that reached Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia. Rājendra’s northern expedition of c. 1021–23 carried Chola arms to the Ganga and brushed against Pāla territory, commemorated in his title Gaṅgaikoṇḍa.

Paramāra of Mālwā

Dhārā · c. 800–1305

A Rajput house famed less for conquest than for culture: the polymath king Bhoja made Dhārā a celebrated centre of Sanskrit learning, architecture and scholarship.

Solankī (Chaulukya)

Aṇhilvāḍa, Gujarat · c. 940–1244

Rulers of a prosperous, trade-rich Gujarat. The dynasty supplied a princess, Durlabhādevī, to the Pāla house and patronised the great Jain temple-building of Mount Ābu and Modhera.

Chāhamāna

Śākambharī · c. 800–1192

A Rajput dynasty of eastern Rajasthan, later remembered as the Chauhans of Ajmer. Their long frontier role ended with the defeat of Pṛthvīrāja III at the second battle of Tarāïn.

Eastern Gaṅga

Kaliṅga / Odisha · c. 1078–1434

Rulers of Kaliṅga who outlasted the Pālas by nearly three centuries. Anantavarman Chodagaṅga unified Odisha and began the Jagannātha temple at Purī, anchoring a durable regional identity.

Pāṇḍya

Madurai · revived c. 1190

An ancient Tamil dynasty long eclipsed by the Cholas. Its later revival under the Pāṇḍyas of Madurai filled the vacuum as Chola power waned.

Chera / Kerala

Malabar coast · c. 800–1102

The Chera (Kulaśekhara) line of the Malabar coast, whose maritime trade tied Kerala to the Arab world and beyond, even as it bore the brunt of Chola naval pressure.

Kashmir

Karkoṭa · Utpala · Lohara — c. 625–1339

A self-contained mountain kingdom whose history is uniquely well documented by Kalhaṇa’s chronicle, the Rājataraṅgiṇī — a rare narrative source for the period.

Hindu Shāhi

Kabul & Gandhāra · c. 850–1026

The frontier kingdom of the north-west, the subcontinent’s shield against forces from beyond the Hindu Kush. It was extinguished by the Ghaznavids around 1026.

Hoysaḷa

S. Karnataka · c. 1026–1343

A power that rose only in the last Pāla century, emerging from the wreck of Chālukya authority to build the celebrated star-shaped temples of the southern Deccan.

Siṃhala (Sri Lanka)

Anurādhapura, then Poḷonnaruwa · to 1232

The island kingdom, repeatedly invaded by the Cholas — who annexed it for a time — before its restoration under Vijayabāhu I and a cultural golden age at Poḷonnaruwa.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

Three forces shown at the edges of the map were not Indian kingdoms in the ordinary sense, but they shaped the Pāla world all the same — by trade, by raid, and by religion.

The Arab Amīrates of Sindh

After the conquest of 711, Sindh became a frontier of the Islamic world, governed from Manṣūra and Multān. Its amīrates pressed on Gujarat and Rajasthan — pressure the Gurjara-Pratihāras were credited with containing — and kept the lower Indus tied into Indian Ocean commerce.

The Ghaznavid Raids

From around 1000, Maḥmūd of Ghazni led a series of campaigns into the north-west and the plains, destroying the Hindu Shāhis and sacking Kanauj. The raids did not reach Bengal, but by shattering the Pratihāra order they reshaped the balance of power the later Pālas inherited.

Pagan & Maritime Asia

The Kingdom of Pagan in Burma, consolidated through the eleventh century, was part of a Buddhist world that looked to the Pāla monasteries for teachers and texts. Pāla artistic styles travelled east by sea, leaving a clear imprint on the Buddhist art of Burma, Tibet and Java.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

The map is political, but the Pāla age is remembered at least as much for what it built and taught. Under Pāla patronage eastern India became the last great stronghold of Buddhism in the subcontinent and a magnet for scholars from across Asia.

The Mahāvihāras

Nālandā, already ancient, flourished anew; Vikramaśilā and Odantapurī were founded or enlarged by Pāla kings. These were international monastic universities — residential, endowed, drawing students from Tibet, China and Southeast Asia. They were also the seedbed of the tantric Vajrayāna that the dynasty did so much to spread.

Art & Architecture

The Pāla school of bronze casting and black-stone sculpture set a standard imitated for centuries. Its iconography travelled with monks and merchants, shaping Buddhist art in Nepal, Tibet and beyond — one of early medieval India’s most far-reaching cultural exports.

A Wider Renaissance

Beyond Bengal, the same centuries saw Bhoja’s Sanskrit academy at Dhārā, the temple-cities of the Cholas and Hoysaḷas, the Jain shrines of Solankī Gujarat, and Kalhaṇa’s history-writing in Kashmir — a subcontinent unusually rich in learning and building.

VI

A Note on Method & the Composite Map

No single map can be true to four centuries of shifting frontiers. The boundaries drawn here are therefore a composite: each kingdom is shown at roughly its characteristic extent during the period of its strength, even though no two of those peaks coincided in the same year. The Pāla field, for instance, reflects the broad reach of the dynasty rather than its much-reduced ninth-century borders.

What is well attested and what is approximate should be kept distinct. Capitals and major cities — Pāṭaliputra, Kanauj, Mānyakheṭa, Thañjāvūr, Dhārā — are securely located. Dynastic dates rest on inscriptions and chronicles and are reliable to within a few years. Territorial fields, by contrast, are interpretive: early medieval kingship was exercised over people, routes and tribute more than over surveyed lines, so every coloured area should be read as a sphere of influence, not a fixed border. The marriage alliances shown with the Pālas follow the reconstruction of O. P. Verma and other scholars and are flagged where the genealogy is debated.

VII

Sources & Further Reading

Primary & Epigraphic Sources

  • Pāla copper-plate grants and stone inscriptions — the Khalimpur, Monghyr and Nālandā plates among them — collected in Epigraphia Indica and the Inscriptions of Bengal.
  • Kalhaṇa, Rājataraṅgiṇī — the twelfth-century verse chronicle of the kings of Kashmir.
  • Sandhyākara Nandī, Rāmacarita — a court poem narrating the reign of Rāmapāla.
  • Tāranātha, History of Buddhism in India — a Tibetan account preserving Pāla monastic tradition.

Modern Scholarship

  • R. C. Majumdar, History of Ancient Bengal.

  • R. S. Sharma, Indian Feudalism and Early Medieval Indian Society.
  • Susan L. Huntington, The “Pāla-Sena” Schools of Sculpture.
  • Hermann Kulke & Dietmar Rothermund, A History of India.
  • O. P. Verma, on Pāla matrimonial alliances (followed for the marriage ties shown here).
  • Abraham Eraly, The First Spring: The Golden Age of India.
