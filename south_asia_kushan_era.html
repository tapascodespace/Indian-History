The Crossroads Empire — India under the Kushans

The kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 30 – c. 375 CE — Silk-Road trade, Gandhāra art, and Buddhism's spread into Central Asia
I

The Kushan Age

The Kushan Empire was one of the great crossroads states of the ancient world — an empire born in the Central Asian steppe that came to straddle the Hindu Kush, ruling from Bactria to the heart of the Gangetic plain. The Kushans were a branch of the Yuezhi, a nomadic confederation displaced from the Gansu corridor by the Xiongnu. By the first century CE, one of the five Yuezhi clans — the Guishang (Kushan) — had unified the others and, under Kujula Kadphises, begun expanding south of the Hindu Kush into Gandhāra and the Indo-Gangetic plain.

It was under Kanishka I (c. 127 CE by the most widely accepted chronology) that the empire reached its zenith. Kanishka's domain stretched from the Aral Sea to Varanasi, encompassing the two greatest artistic workshops of the ancient Indian world — Gandhāra (Graeco-Buddhist sculpture) and Mathurā (indigenous Indian sculpture) — and the lucrative Silk Road trade connecting Rome, Persia, China, and India.

The map below shows the Kushan world at its peak — the vast empire linking Central and South Asia, flanked by the Satavāhanas of the Deccan, the Western Kshatrapas in Gujarat, and the Indo-Parthian remnants on the northwestern fringe.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Kushan Empire

Puruṣapura & Mathurā · c. 30–375

Founded by Kujula Kadphises, the Kushan Empire reached its peak under Kanishka I, whose Buddhist council at Kashmir and patronage of Gandhāra art made him one of the most celebrated rulers of the ancient world. Kushan coins — struck in gold, copying Roman weight standards — show deities from Greek, Iranian, Hindu, and Buddhist pantheons, reflecting the empire's cosmopolitan character. The later Kushans (the "Little Kushans") survived as vassals and were eventually absorbed by the Sasanians and Guptas.

Sātavāhana

Pratiṣṭhāna · c. 100 BCE–220 CE

The first great Deccan empire, ruling from Pratiṣṭhāna (Paithan) on the Godavari. The Sātavāhanas controlled the crucial east-west trade routes across the peninsula and patronised Buddhism — the great stūpas at Amarāvatī and Nāgārjunakoṇḍa are monuments of their era. Their long rivalry with the Western Kshatrapas shaped the political geography of western India for centuries.

Western Kshatrapa

Ujjain · c. 35–405 CE

Saka rulers who controlled western India from Ujjain and the Gujarat coast. Their most notable king, Rudradāman I, left the Junāgaḍh rock inscription (c. 150 CE) — the first long inscription in Sanskrit prose, recording the repair of the Sudarśana lake. The Kshatrapas issued a prolific silver coinage and controlled the lucrative western Indian ports.

Indo-Parthian

Seistan & Sindh · c. 19–226 CE

The Indo-Parthians were Arsacid-related rulers who held territory in eastern Iran and the lower Indus valley. Gondophares (c. 19–46 CE) was their most notable king, linked in Christian tradition to the apostle Thomas. They were eventually absorbed by the expanding Kushan Empire and the Sasanians.

Sangam Tamil Kingdoms

Tamilakam · c. 300 BCE–300 CE

The three ancient Tamil kingdoms — Chera, Chola, and Pāṇḍya — are known from the Sangam literary corpus, one of the oldest bodies of secular literature in India. Roman coins found across Tamil Nadu attest to the vibrant Indian Ocean trade of this era, connecting the Tamil ports to the Roman Mediterranean.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

Rome & the Indian Ocean Trade

The Kushan period coincided with Rome's peak engagement with India. Roman gold flowed to the subcontinent through the Red Sea trade and overland through Parthia. The Periplus of the Erythraean Sea (c. 40–70 CE) describes the ports, goods, and currencies of this trade in detail.

Han China & the Silk Road

The Kushan Empire sat at the midpoint of the Silk Road, mediating trade between Han China and Rome. Chinese envoys like Ban Chao engaged with Kushan rulers, and Chinese silk reached Rome through Kushan intermediaries. Buddhism also travelled eastward along these routes into Central Asia and China.

Sasanian Persia

The rise of the Sasanian dynasty in Iran from 224 CE gradually compressed Kushan territory from the west. Sasanian kings conquered Bactria and parts of Gandhāra, reducing the later Kushans to subordinate "Kushano-Sasanian" governors — a transition visible in the hybrid coinage of the period.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

Gandhāra & Mathurā Art

The Kushan period produced two of the greatest art schools of the ancient world. Gandhāra fused Hellenistic and Indian traditions to create the first anthropomorphic images of the Buddha — a revolution in religious art. Mathurā, working in red sandstone in a purely Indian idiom, produced parallel Buddha images as well as iconic Jain and Hindu sculpture.

Buddhism's Transformation

Under Kushan patronage, Mahāyāna Buddhism emerged as a distinct tradition. Kanishka's Buddhist council helped codify texts, and the philosopher Nāgārjuna developed the Mādhyamaka philosophy of emptiness — one of the most influential philosophical systems in Asian history. Buddhism's spread along the Silk Road into China, Korea, and Japan began in earnest during this era.

Science & Medicine

The physician Charaka, associated with Kanishka's court, compiled the Charaka Saṃhitā, one of the foundational texts of Ayurvedic medicine. Indian astronomy and mathematics continued to develop, and the interaction between Greek, Iranian, and Indian knowledge systems at the Kushan crossroads fostered innovation in all three traditions.

VI

A Note on Method

The Kushan Empire spanned over three centuries and its frontiers shifted dramatically. This map shows the empire at roughly its peak under Kanishka I (c. 127–150 CE), when it extended from Bactria to eastern Uttar Pradesh. The contemporaries shown — Sātavāhana, Western Kshatrapa, Indo-Parthian — are also shown at their approximate extents during this period.

All territorial boundaries should be read as spheres of influence. The Kushan state was a loose confederation rather than a centrally administered empire; local rulers retained considerable autonomy under imperial suzerainty.

VII

Sources & Further Reading

Primary Sources

  • Kushan gold and copper coin series — the primary source for Kushan royal names, titles, and religious affiliations.
  • Rabatak inscription of Kanishka — key for establishing the Kushan genealogy and dating.
  • Junāgaḍh rock inscription of Rudradāman I (c. 150 CE) — earliest long Sanskrit inscription.
  • Periplus of the Erythraean Sea (c. 40–70 CE) — Greek navigational guide describing Indian Ocean trade.
  • Sangam literature — Pattuppāṭṭu and Eṭṭuttokai anthologies for Tamil kingdoms.

Modern Scholarship

  • Romila Thapar, The Penguin History of Early India.
  • Upinder Singh, A History of Ancient and Early Medieval India.
  • H. Falk, "The Kanishka Era in Gupta Records" — for Kushan chronological debates.
