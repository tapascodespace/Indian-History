The kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 1720 – c. 1800 CE — the Sikh Empire, Ahom, Mysore under Tipu, Hyderabad, Awadh, and Bengal
I

The Eighteenth-Century Mosaic

The collapse of Mughal authority after Aurangzeb’s death (1707 CE) produced a kaleidoscope of successor states across the subcontinent. By the mid-eighteenth century, effective power lay not with the Mughal emperor in Delhi but with a constellation of regional powers — each with its own court, army, revenue system, and cultural patronage.

This plate maps the major independent and semi-independent states of the late eighteenth century: the Sikh Confederacy in the Punjab (which would become Ranjit Singh’s empire), Mysore under Hyder Ali and Tipu Sultan, the Nizam of Hyderabad, the Nawab of Awadh, the Ahom kingdom of Assam, and the Bengal Nawabs (whose defeat at Plassey in 1757 began the British conquest of India).

This is the India on the eve of colonialism — a diverse, politically fragmented subcontinent where the growing power of the British East India Company was about to transform the political landscape entirely.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Sikh Empire

Lahore · c. 1799–1849 CE

Ranjit Singh unified the Sikh misls (confederacies) into a powerful kingdom stretching from the Sutlej to the Khyber Pass. His army — trained by European officers and equipped with modern artillery — was the last Indian force capable of meeting the British on equal terms. The Anglo-Sikh Wars (1845–49) ended Sikh independence.

Mysore under Hyder Ali & Tipu Sultan

Seringapatam · c. 1761–1799 CE

Hyder Ali and his son Tipu Sultan (the “Tiger of Mysore”) created the most formidable military state in southern India. Tipu introduced rocket artillery, modernised administration, and sought French alliance against the British. His death at Seringapatam (1799) ended Mysorean resistance. The four Anglo-Mysore Wars were among the most hard-fought of the colonial era.

Nizam of Hyderabad

Hyderabad · c. 1724–1948 CE

The Āsaf Jāhi dynasty ruled the largest princely state in India from 1724 until Indian independence. Originally a Mughal governor who became independent, the Nizam presided over a multi-religious state with a distinctive Indo-Islamic culture. Hyderabad eventually accepted British subsidiary alliance (1798).

Ahom Kingdom of Assam

Sibsagar · c. 1228–1826 CE

The Ahom kingdom of Assam was one of the longest-ruling dynasties in Indian history — six centuries of continuous rule. They successfully repelled Mughal invasions (the Battle of Saraighat, 1671) and maintained independence until the Burmese invasion of 1817 and British annexation in 1826.

Nawabs of Awadh

Lucknow · c. 1722–1856 CE

The Nawabs of Awadh (Oudh) created a refined court culture at Lucknow — famous for its architecture, cuisine, music, and Urdu poetry. Under British subsidiary alliance from 1765, Awadh gradually lost independence before outright annexation in 1856 — a grievance that fueled the 1857 Revolt.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

The British East India Company

From its base in Bengal (won at Plassey in 1757), the British East India Company systematically extended control through warfare, subsidiary alliances, and the doctrine of lapse. By 1800, the Company controlled Bengal, the Carnatic, and much of the east coast, and was the arbiter of Indian politics.

Afghan & Persian Invasions

Nader Shah’s sack of Delhi (1739) and Ahmad Shah Durrani’s invasions (1748–67) demonstrated the Mughal empire’s helplessness and reshaped the political landscape of northern India. The Sikh Confederacy emerged partly in response to Afghan depredations in the Punjab.

French Influence

French officers trained and commanded Indian armies across the subcontinent — in Hyderabad, Mysore, and the Maratha states. The Anglo-French rivalry in India (a theatre of global wars) ended with British supremacy after the Fourth Anglo-Mysore War (1799) and the fall of French-trained Indian armies.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

Lucknow & Awadhi Culture

The Nawabs of Awadh created one of the most refined court cultures in India. Lucknow became the centre of Urdu poetry, Kathak dance, and a distinctive architectural style. The nawabi cuisine — biriyani, kebāb, elaborate dum cooking — remains a defining element of North Indian food culture.

Sikh Architecture & Scripture

The Harmandir Sahib (Golden Temple) at Amritsar, rebuilt and gilded under Ranjit Singh, is the supreme monument of Sikh devotional architecture. The Guru Granth Sahib — compiled in the sixteenth century but central to eighteenth-century Sikh identity — is one of the world’s great religious texts.

Military Modernisation

The eighteenth century saw Indian states adopt European military technology: Tipu’s rockets, Scindia’s French-trained infantry, and Ranjit Singh’s Khalsa army (with European drill and artillery) represent the most sophisticated Indian military responses to the colonial challenge before their eventual defeat.

VI

A Note on Method

This plate shows the fragmented political landscape of the late eighteenth century (c. 1780 CE) — after Mughal collapse but before British supremacy was established. Multiple independent powers are shown simultaneously.

This is the most politically complex plate in the series. No single state controlled the subcontinent; the map should be read as a mosaic of coexisting powers rather than a territorial empire. The Maratha Confederacy (see previous plate) overlapped with several of these states.
