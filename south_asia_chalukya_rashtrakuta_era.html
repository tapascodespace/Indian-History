The Deccan Powers — Chālukyas & Rāṣṭrakūṭas

The kingdoms of the Deccan, c. 543 – c. 982 CE — Bādāmi, Kalyāṇī, and the Rāṣṭrakūṭa imperial age
I

The Deccan Powers

For nearly five centuries, the Deccan was dominated by two great dynasties — the Chālukyas (in two branches: early Bādāmi and later Kalyāṇī) and the Rāṣṭrakūṭas of Mānyakheṭa, who ruled between them. These were the superpowers of medieval India, capable of projecting force from the Narmada to the Kaveri and, in the case of the Rāṣṭrakūṭas, campaigning as far as Kanauj in the north.

The Bādāmi Chālukyas (c. 543–757) under Pulakeshin II famously defeated Harsha and dominated the Deccan until the Rāṣṭrakūṭas overthrew them. The Rāṣṭrakūṭas (c. 753–982) built the Kailāsa temple at Ellora — the world's largest monolithic sculpture — and were described by Arab travellers as one of the four great empires of the world. The Kalyāṇī Chālukyas (c. 973–1189) then revived the Chālukya lineage.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Bādāmi Chālukya

Bādāmi · c. 543–757 CE

Founded by Pulakeshin I, the early Chālukyas made Bādāmi (Vātāpi) their fortress-capital. Pulakeshin II (r. c. 610–642) defeated Harsha's northern campaign and received a diplomatic mission from the Sasanian emperor. The temples at Aihole (the "cradle of Indian architecture") and Paṭṭadakal (a UNESCO World Heritage Site) are their architectural legacy.

Rāṣṭrakūṭa

Mānyakheṭa · c. 753–982 CE

Dantidurga overthrew the last Bādāmi Chālukya and founded the Rāṣṭrakūṭa dynasty, which became perhaps the most powerful Indian dynasty of the ninth century. Amoghavarsha I was a scholar-king; Krishna I commissioned the Kailāsa temple at Ellora — carved top-down from a single basalt cliff. Arab travellers ranked the Rāṣṭrakūṭas alongside the Abbasids, Byzantines, and Tang Chinese as the four great empires of the world.

Kalyāṇī Chālukya

Kalyāṇī · c. 973–1189 CE

The "Western" or "Later" Chālukyas revived the dynasty after overthrowing the Rāṣṭrakūṭas. Under Vikramāditya VI, they issued the Chālukya-Vikrama era calendar. Their feudatories — Hoysaḷa, Kākatīya, Yādava — would eventually become independent and dominate the later Deccan.

Pallava

Kāñchīpuram · c. 275–897 CE

The Pallavas were the Chālukyas' primary southern rival. The Pallava–Chālukya wars — the sack of Bādāmi by Narasimhavarman I, the retaliatory campaigns — defined the political geography of peninsular India for two centuries. See the dedicated Pallava plate for details.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

The Tripartite Struggle for Kanauj

The ninth century saw a three-way struggle for the symbolic prize of Kanauj between the Rāṣṭrakūṭas, the Gurjara-Pratīhāras, and the Pāla dynasty of Bengal. The Rāṣṭrakūṭas sacked Kanauj multiple times but could not hold it permanently — the northern campaigns overstretched their resources.

Arab & Islamic World

The Rāṣṭrakūṭa period coincided with the expansion of Islam. Arab merchants were active in the western Indian ports, and the Arab geographer Sulaiman (c. 851 CE) described the Rāṣṭrakūṭa king as one of the four great kings of the world. The Rāṣṭrakūṭas maintained tolerant relations with Arab traders.

Southeast Asian Connections

Deccan dynasties maintained cultural connections with Southeast Asia through trade and Buddhist/Hindu religious networks. The Chālukya and Rāṣṭrakūṭa artistic traditions influenced temple architecture in Java and Cambodia.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

Ellora & Elephanta

The rock-cut temples at Ellora — Hindu, Buddhist, and Jain — are the crowning achievement of Indian rock architecture. The Kailāsa temple (Cave 16), commissioned by Rāṣṭrakūṭa Krishna I, is the world's largest monolithic sculpture — carved from the top down to simulate a free-standing Dravidian temple. Elephanta Island's Trimurti Shiva is another Rāṣṭrakūṭa masterpiece.

Kannada & Sanskrit Literature

The Rāṣṭrakūṭa court patronised both Kannada and Sanskrit literature. Amoghavarsha I himself composed the earliest extant Kannada prose work, Kavirājamārga. The poet Pampa (c. 941 CE) produced the Vikramārjunavijaya, one of the great Kannada epics.

Temple Architecture: Aihole to Paṭṭadakal

Aihole has been called the "cradle of Indian temple architecture" — its 120+ temples show experiments in every early structural form (Nāgara, Drāviḍa, and hybrid). Paṭṭadakal, a UNESCO World Heritage Site, showcases the mature Chālukya style with its Virūpākṣa and Mallikārjuna temples.

A Note on Method

This plate covers nearly five centuries of Deccan history under three successive dynasties that controlled roughly the same territory. The map shows the maximum extent of the Rāṣṭrakūṭa period (c. 850 CE), when the Deccan empire's reach was greatest.

The Bādāmi Chālukya, Rāṣṭrakūṭa, and Kalyāṇī Chālukya domains overlapped significantly; the coloured region represents the combined Deccan core controlled by whichever dynasty was ascendant at a given time.
