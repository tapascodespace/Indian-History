A Historical Cartography

North India in the Age of Harshavardhana

The kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 600 – c. 650 CE — the last great northern empire before early-medieval fragmentation
I

The Age of Harsha

For barely half a century — yet with an intensity that left a lasting mark on Indian memory — Harshavardhana of Thānesar and Kanauj united much of the northern plains under a single sovereign. Coming to power around 606 CE after the murder of his brother Rājyavardhana, Harsha spent the first years of his reign in almost continuous campaigning, absorbing the Maukhari domain, subduing Gauḍa in Bengal, and pressing westward into Gujarat and southward to the Narmadā.

His empire was the last to govern the Indo-Gangetic heartland as a unitary state before the subcontinent fragmented into the regional kingdoms of the early-medieval centuries — the very world the Pāla plate in this series depicts. Harsha's fame rests not only on conquest but on culture: his court poet Bāṇabhaṭṭa composed the Harṣacarita and Kādambarī, two landmarks of Sanskrit prose, while the Chinese pilgrim Xuanzang spent years travelling his dominions, leaving in his Xiyuji the most detailed outsider account of early-medieval India.

The map below shows the political world of Harsha's reign — his own vast but loosely held empire at its centre, flanked by the Chālukyas of Bādāmi under Pulakeśin II to the south, the Maitrakas of Valabhī in Gujarat, the kingdom of Kāmarūpa in Assam, and the shadow of the recently fallen Gupta order still visible in the successor states of the Gangetic plain.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

The side panel lists every power on the map with its dates and colour. The cards below add the narrative a historian would need — who each dynasty was, why it mattered, and how it stood in relation to Harsha's empire.

Harsha's Empire

Thānesar & Kanauj · c. 606–647

Son of Prabhākaravardhana of the Puṣyabhūti dynasty of Thānesar, Harsha came to power after his brother Rājyavardhana was treacherously killed by Śaśāṅka of Gauḍa. Over the next six years he united the Maukhari domain of Kanauj with his own, subdued Bengal, and extended his sway from Gujarat to Kāmarūpa. His court was a centre of Sanskrit letters — Bāṇabhaṭṭa composed both the Harṣacarita and the Kādambarī there — and he convened the famous assembly at Prayāga. His death without an heir in 647 left a power vacuum that would not be filled for a century.

Chālukya of Bādāmi

Bādāmi · c. 543–753

The early Chālukyas of Bādāmi were the dominant Deccan power. Pulakeśin II famously defeated Harsha on the banks of the Narmadā around 618–620 CE, halting the northern emperor's southward expansion. Pulakeśin himself was later killed when the Pallava king Narasiṃhavarman I sacked Bādāmi in 642.

Pallava

Kāñchī · c. 275–897

The Pallavas of Kāñchīpuram were the leading power of the Tamil south. Under Mahendravarman I and his son Narasiṃhavarman I, they challenged the Chālukyas and built the extraordinary rock-cut temples of Mahābalipuram. Their court was a bridge between Sanskrit and Tamil literary traditions.

Maitraka of Valabhī

Valabhī, Gujarat · c. 475–776

A Gujarati dynasty that maintained independence through diplomacy, hosting both Buddhist and Brahmanical learning. Valabhī was a renowned centre of education, rivalling Nālandā, and a major port on the Arabian Sea trade routes. The Maitrakas were allied to Harsha through marriage.

Kāmarūpa

Prāgjyotiṣa (Assam) · c. 350–1140

The kingdom of Kāmarūpa in the Brahmaputra valley was ruled by Bhāskaravarman, Harsha's close ally against Śaśāṅka of Gauḍa. Xuanzang visited Bhāskaravarman's court and left a vivid description. After Bhāskaravarman's death without heirs, the kingdom passed to other lines.

Gauḍa under Śaśāṅka

Bengal · c. 590–637

Śaśāṅka, ruler of Gauḍa in Bengal, was Harsha's great antagonist. Buddhist sources accuse him of destroying the Bodhi tree at Bodh Gayā and persecuting Buddhists. His murder of Rājyavardhana triggered Harsha's rise; after Śaśāṅka's death around 637, Gauḍa fell into a period of anarchy — the mātsyanyāya — that ended only with the election of Gopāla I and the founding of the Pāla dynasty.

Later Gupta

Magadha · c. 490–750

Not direct descendants of the imperial Guptas, this line ruled a reduced Magadha as vassals and later as minor sovereigns. They were absorbed into Harsha's growing dominion and thereafter survived as subordinate rulers.

Western Gaṅga

Talakāḍ · c. 350–1000

A Karnataka dynasty that served as a buffer between the Chālukyas and the Pallavas. The Western Gaṅgas were notable patrons of Jainism and erected some of the earliest Kannada inscriptions.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

Three external forces shaped Harsha's world — by pilgrimage, trade, and the shadow of empire.

Tang China & Xuanzang

The Chinese monk Xuanzang arrived in India around 630 and spent years at Nālandā and in Harsha's court. His Great Tang Records on the Western Regions (Xiyuji) provides the most detailed account of seventh-century India — its kingdoms, monasteries, and beliefs. Harsha himself exchanged embassies with the Tang emperor Taizong.

The Sasanian & Arab Worlds

The western Indian ports traded actively with Persia and, increasingly, with the expanding Arab world. The fall of the Sasanian Empire to the Arab conquest in 651 — just four years after Harsha's death — would eventually reshape the subcontinent's north-western frontier.

Southeast Asian Kingdoms

Indian cultural influence — Buddhist and Hindu — continued to flow to the kingdoms of Dvāravatī, Śrīvijaya, and the Khmer. Harsha's era coincided with a great age of temple-building in mainland and maritime Southeast Asia, carried by monks, merchants, and Brahmin advisors from India.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

Harsha's age was one of the high points of Sanskrit literature and Buddhist learning — a bridge between the Gupta classical period and the early-medieval regional cultures.

Sanskrit Letters

Bāṇabhaṭṭa's Harṣacarita is the first historical biography in Sanskrit, while his Kādambarī is one of the longest and most ornate prose romances in any language. Harsha himself is credited with three plays — the Ratnāvalī, Priyadarśikā, and Nāgānanda. The court also sustained grammarians and philosophers.

Nālandā & Buddhist Learning

Nālandā, already centuries old, reached one of its peaks under Harsha's patronage. Xuanzang studied there for years and described a community of thousands of monks debating Mahāyāna, Hīnayāna, and Brahmanical philosophy. Harsha's grand assemblies at Prayāga and Kanauj showcased both Buddhist and Hindu scholarship.

Temple Art & Architecture

In the south, Mahendravarman I of the Pallavas pioneered rock-cut temple architecture; his son Narasiṃhavarman I commissioned the shore temples and rathas of Mahābalipuram. In the Deccan, the Chālukya temples at Aihole and Bādāmi established a new architectural vocabulary. Together, these represent the formative age of Indian stone temple construction.

VI

A Note on Method & the Composite Map

Harsha's reign lasted barely forty years, making this map more closely tied to a single moment than most plates in this series. Yet even within those decades, frontiers shifted: Gauḍa, for instance, was independent under Śaśāṅka until about 637 and then absorbed into Harsha's domain. The map therefore shows approximate spheres at their greatest extent.

Capitals and major cities — Kanauj, Thānesar, Bādāmi, Kāñchī, Valabhī — are securely located by inscriptions, coins, and Xuanzang's account. Territorial fields are interpretive: Harsha's "empire" was a patchwork of direct rule, tributary relationships, and nominal suzerainty rather than a uniform state. Every coloured area should be read as a sphere of influence, not a surveyed border.

VII

Sources & Further Reading

Primary & Literary Sources

  • Bāṇabhaṭṭa, Harṣacarita — the court biography of Harshavardhana, the principal Sanskrit narrative source.
  • Xuanzang, Da Tang Xiyu Ji (Great Tang Records on the Western Regions) — the Chinese pilgrim's account of seventh-century India.
  • Harsha's Bānskhera and Madhuban copper-plate inscriptions — land grants providing administrative and genealogical data.
  • Aihole inscription of Pulakeśin II (634 CE) — records his victory over Harsha at the Narmadā.

Modern Scholarship

  • R. K. Mookerji, Harsha — the standard modern biography.
  • Romila Thapar, The Penguin History of Early India: From the Origins to AD 1300.
  • Hermann Kulke & Dietmar Rothermund, A History of India.
  • D. Devahuti, Harsha: A Political Study.
  • S. Beal (tr.), Si-Yu-Ki: Buddhist Records of the Western World — Xuanzang's narrative in English.
