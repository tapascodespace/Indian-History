The First Empire — India under the Mauryas

The kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 322 – c. 185 BCE — the first subcontinental empire, the Aśokan edicts and Dhamma
I

The Mauryan Empire

The Maurya Empire was the first to unite nearly the entire Indian subcontinent under a single political authority. Founded by Chandragupta Maurya around 322 BCE — who seized power from the Nanda dynasty of Magadha and then turned to expel the Macedonian garrisons left by Alexander — the empire reached its greatest extent under his grandson Aśoka (r. c. 268–232 BCE), whose rock and pillar edicts have been found from Afghanistan to Karnataka.

Aśoka’s conversion to Buddhism after the bloody conquest of Kaliṅga (c. 261 BCE) is one of the most famous episodes in Indian history. His edicts — carved in Brāhmī, Kharoṣṭhī, Greek, and Aramaic — proclaim a policy of dhamma (righteous rule) that emphasised tolerance, non-violence, and moral governance. The Mauryan administration, described in Kauṭilya’s Arthaśāstra, was the most sophisticated bureaucratic system ancient India produced.

The map below shows the Mauryan world at its peak under Aśoka — the vast empire stretching from Afghanistan to Bengal and southward to Karnataka, with the unconquered Tamil kingdoms beyond its southern frontier.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Maurya Empire

Pāṭaliputra · c. 322–185 BCE

Chandragupta Maurya overthrew the Nanda dynasty, defeated the Macedonian satrapies, and concluded a treaty with Seleucus Nicator that ceded the trans-Indus territories to the Mauryas. His son Bindusāra extended control southward. Aśoka completed the conquest of the subcontinent with the brutal subjugation of Kaliṅga (c. 261 BCE) — an event that, by his own account, filled him with remorse and prompted his embrace of dhamma. Aśoka’s edicts, found across the subcontinent, are the earliest readable inscriptions in Indian history.

The Tamil Kingdoms

Tamilakam · ancient

The Chera, Chola, and Pāṇḍya kingdoms of the far south remained beyond Mauryan control. Aśoka’s Rock Edict II mentions them by name as neighbouring peoples to whom he sent missions of dhamma. These kingdoms would endure for over a millennium, producing some of the richest literary and architectural traditions of the subcontinent.

The Seleucid Frontier

c. 305–250 BCE

Seleucus I Nicator attempted to reconquer Alexander’s Indian territories but was defeated by Chandragupta. The resulting treaty ceded Gandhāra, Arachosia, and parts of Baluchistan to the Mauryas in exchange for 500 war elephants. Seleucus’ ambassador Megasthenes resided at Pāṭaliputra and wrote the Indikā, a detailed (if sometimes fanciful) account of Mauryan India.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

The Hellenistic World

The Mauryas maintained diplomatic relations with the Hellenistic kingdoms — Seleucids, Ptolemies, and Antigonids. Greek ambassadors like Megasthenes and Deimachus visited Pāṭaliputra. Aśoka’s edicts mention the Greek kings by name as recipients of his dhamma missions — the earliest evidence of Indian diplomatic awareness of the wider Mediterranean world.

Sri Lanka & Buddhist Missions

Aśoka sent his son Mahinda (or brother, in some traditions) to introduce Buddhism to Sri Lanka. The mission was a spectacular success — the island became and remains a bastion of Theravāda Buddhism. Missions were also sent to Central Asia, Southeast Asia, and the Hellenistic world.

Central Asian Frontiers

The Maurya Empire’s northwestern frontier extended into modern Afghanistan. The bilingual edicts of Aśoka at Kandahar (in Greek and Aramaic) and Laghman (in Aramaic) attest to Mauryan control of these regions and the multicultural character of the frontier provinces.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

The Aśokan Edicts

Aśoka’s rock and pillar edicts — carved in Brāhmī, Kharosthi, Greek, and Aramaic — are the earliest substantial body of Indian writing. They articulate a philosophy of governance based on dhamma: tolerance of all religions, non-violence, care for animals, and moral self-improvement. The lion capital of Aśoka’s pillar at Sārnāth is today the emblem of the Republic of India.

Mauryan Administration

Kauṭilya’s Arthaśāstra — whether composed in the Mauryan period or somewhat later — describes a highly centralised bureaucratic state with an elaborate spy network, economic regulation, and provincial governance. Pāṭaliputra itself, described by Megasthenes, was one of the largest cities in the ancient world.

Art & Architecture

Mauryan art introduced polished stone sculpture to India — the Didarganj Yakshi, the Sarnath Lion Capital, the Lomas Rishi cave. The Mauryan polish on stone, achieved through techniques still debated, remains one of the most distinctive features of Indian art history.

VI

A Note on Method

The Maurya Empire lasted about 137 years, and Aśoka’s reign covers only its middle third. The map shows the empire at its peak under Aśoka (c. 260–232 BCE), when his edicts were being carved from Kandahar in the west to Kaliṅga in the east.

The Mauryan Empire was more centralised than most ancient Indian states, but even here “boundaries” are approximate. Aśoka’s edicts themselves suggest that imperial control shaded into influence and then into diplomatic contact as one moved from the Gangetic core to the periphery.

VII

Sources & Further Reading

Primary Sources

  • The Aśokan Rock and Pillar Edicts — the foundational epigraphic source for Mauryan history.
  • Megasthenes, Indikā (fragments preserved in Strabo, Arrian, Diodorus) — a Greek ambassador’s account of Pāṭaliputra.
  • Kauṭilya, Arthaśāstra — a treatise on statecraft attributed to Chandragupta’s minister.
  • Buddhist Pali chronicles: Dīpavaṃsa and Mahāvaṃsa — for Aśoka’s Buddhist missions.

Modern Scholarship

  • Romila Thapar, Aśoka and the Decline of the Mauryas — the standard modern study.
  • Upinder Singh, A History of Ancient and Early Medieval India.
  • Patrick Olivelle (tr. & ed.), King, Governance, and Law in Ancient India: Kauṭilya’s Arthaśāstra.
  • Harry Falk, Aśokan Sites and Artefacts.
