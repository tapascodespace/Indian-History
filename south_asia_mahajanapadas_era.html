The Sixteen Mahājanapadas — India's First Republics & Kingdoms

The kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 600 – c. 345 BCE — the age of the Buddha, Mahāvīra, and the rise of Magadha
I

The Sixteen Mahājanapadas

By the sixth century BCE, the Indian subcontinent had crystallised into sixteen great realms — the mahājanapadas (great footprints of peoples) — stretching from Gandhāra in the northwest to Aṅga in the east. This was the world of the Buddha and Mahāvīra: an age of philosophical revolution, urbanisation, and fierce interstate competition that would culminate in the rise of Magadha as the dominant power of northern India.

These sixteen states took various forms — monarchies like Magadha, Kosala, and Avanti; republics (gaṇasaṅghas) like the Vajjian Confederacy and the Malla; and the ancient oligarchic clans of the northwest. Among them, Magadha — blessed with rich iron-ore deposits, fertile alluvial soil, and strategic location at the eastern Gangetic terminus — gradually consumed its rivals under the Haryaṅka, Śiśunāga, and finally the Nanda dynasties.

The map below shows these sixteen realms approximately as described in Buddhist and Jain literary sources — a patchwork of polities that would, within two centuries, be unified under the Mauryas.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Magadha

Rājagṛha · c. 600–345 BCE

Under the Haryaṅka dynasty — Bimbisāra and his son Ajātashatru — Magadha conquered Aṅga and Kāśī and defeated the Vajjian Confederacy. The Śiśunāgas conquered Avanti. Finally the Nandas built the largest pre-Mauryan army in India, establishing Magadha as the unchallenged power of the Gangetic plain. The Buddha and Mahāvīra both preached in Magadha's territories.

Kosala

Śrāvastī · c. 600–400 BCE

The kingdom of Prasenajit, a contemporary of the Buddha who is mentioned frequently in Buddhist texts. Kosala controlled much of the middle Gangetic plain before being absorbed by Magadha. The city of Śrāvastī (Sāvatthī) was where the Buddha spent many rainy-season retreats.

Vajjian Confederacy

Vaiśālī · c. 600–468 BCE

A republic of eight clans (the Licchavi being the most prominent), the Vajjians were the most celebrated gaṇasaṅgha of ancient India. Their capital Vaiśālī hosted the Second Buddhist Council. They were defeated by Ajātashatru through intrigue and force.

Avanti

Ujjain · c. 600–400 BCE

The western rival of Magadha, ruled by king Pradyota. Avanti controlled the crucial east-west trade routes through central India. Eventually absorbed by the Śiśunāga dynasty of Magadha.

Gandhāra

Taxila · ancient

The great learning centre of Taxila (Takṣaśilā) was Gandhāra's jewel. Under Achaemenid Persian suzerainty from c. 518 BCE, Gandhāra paid tribute to Darius I and served in Persian armies. It remained a cosmopolitan crossroads linking India to Iran and Central Asia.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

Achaemenid Persia

The northwest — Gandhāra and parts of Sindh — was under Achaemenid control from c. 518 BCE. Darius I's Behistun inscription lists Hindush as a satrapy. Persian silver sigloi circulated alongside Indian punch-marked coins in the Punjab. Alexander's invasion (327–325 BCE) would end Achaemenid rule but open the way for the Indo-Greek kingdoms.

Alexander's Invasion

In 327–325 BCE, Alexander of Macedon invaded the northwest, defeating king Porus at the Battle of the Hydaspes. His army refused to march further east — reportedly daunted by reports of the Nanda army. The invasion was brief but left behind Greek garrisons and the seeds of the Indo-Greek kingdoms.

Sri Lanka & the South

The southern subcontinent was beyond the mahājanapada world. The early Tamil Sangam kingdoms were emerging in the far south, and Sri Lanka's Vijayan settlement tradition places the island's founding in this era. Indian Ocean trade routes were already active.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

The Buddha & Mahāvīra

The sixth century BCE saw the birth of Buddhism and Jainism. Siddhārtha Gautama (the Buddha) and Vardhamāna Mahāvīra (the Jina) both preached in the Magadhan world, challenging Brahmanical orthodoxy with new ethical and philosophical systems. Their teachings would reshape Asian civilisation.

Urbanisation & Trade

The mahājanapada period saw rapid urbanisation — the cities of Rājagṛha, Vaiśālī, Śrāvastī, Kauśāmbī, and Taxila are archaeological landmarks. Punch-marked silver coins appeared, indicating monetised trade. The Northern Black Polished Ware (NBPW) is the diagnostic ceramic of this urban revolution.

Early Sanskrit Grammar

Pāṇini's Aṣṭādhyāyī, composed in the Gandhāra region around the fifth century BCE, is the most comprehensive grammar of any ancient language — a masterpiece of linguistic analysis. It describes the Sanskrit language with algorithmic precision and remains the foundation of Sanskrit grammar to this day.

VI

A Note on Method

The sixteen mahājanapadas are a literary construct — listed in Buddhist texts (the Aṅguttara Nikāya) and Jain texts with slight variations. Their exact boundaries are unknown; the map shows approximate locations based on archaeological evidence and textual references.

The period covers roughly 250 years (c. 600–345 BCE) during which Magadha progressively absorbed its rivals. The map shows the mid-point of this process, with Magadha expanded but the other states not yet fully absorbed.
