The Maratha Confederacy — Peshwas & Great Houses

The kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 1674 – c. 1818 CE — Shivaji’s swarajya, the Peshwa ascendancy, and the great Maratha houses
I

The Maratha Confederacy

The Maratha rise is one of the most remarkable stories in Indian history. From Shivaji’s guerrilla campaigns against Bijapur and the Mughals in the 1650s–70s, a small Deccan chieftaincy grew into the dominant military power of eighteenth-century India — a confederacy whose armies campaigned from Attock on the Indus to Thanjavur in the south, and from Surat on the Arabian Sea to Cuttack on the Bay of Bengal.

After Shivaji’s death (1680 CE) and the Mughal-Maratha wars, real power passed to the Peshwas (prime ministers) of Pune, who directed a confederacy of great houses: the Gaekwads of Baroda, Holkars of Indore, Scindias of Gwalior, and the Bhonsles of Nagpur. This confederacy was the principal successor to Mughal power in India, before being broken by the British in the Third Anglo-Maratha War (1817–18).

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Shivaji Maharaj

Raigad · r. 1674–1680 CE

Shivaji carved an independent kingdom from Bijapur and Mughal territory through brilliant guerrilla warfare and strategic fort-building. His coronation in 1674 CE as Chhatrapati (sovereign emperor) established the Maratha claim to independent sovereignty. His administrative innovations — a navy, fort-based defense system, and revenue administration — created the institutional foundations of Maratha power.

Peshwa Era

Pune · c. 1713–1818 CE

Under the Peshwas — especially Bājī Rāo I (r. 1720–1740) — the Marathas expanded explosively across India. Bājī Rāo never lost a battle, campaigning from Delhi to the Deccan. The Third Battle of Panipat (1761) against Ahmad Shah Durrani was a devastating defeat but did not end Maratha power — the confederacy recovered and remained the dominant Indian power until the British wars.

Scindia of Gwalior

Gwalior · c. 1731–1948

Mahadji Scindia (r. 1761–1794) was the most powerful Indian ruler of the late eighteenth century — controlling the Mughal emperor in Delhi as his puppet, modernising his army with European-trained battalions, and dominating northern India until his death. His French-officered army was the most formidable in India before British supremacy.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

The British Challenge

From the 1770s, the British East India Company emerged as the Marathas’ principal rival. Three Anglo-Maratha Wars (1775–82, 1803–05, 1817–18) progressively dismantled the confederacy. The Third War ended with the Peshwa’s surrender and British supremacy over the subcontinent.

Afghan Invasions

Ahmad Shah Durrani’s invasions (1748–67) and the catastrophic Third Battle of Panipat (1761) devastated Maratha power in the north. Over 100,000 Marathas died at Panipat — one of the deadliest single battles in history. The Marathas recovered but never regained their pre-1761 dominance.

European Powers

The French, Portuguese, and British all played roles in Maratha politics. French officers trained Maratha armies (especially Scindia’s); the Portuguese held Goa; and the British gradually used diplomacy, subsidiaries, and force to break Maratha unity.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

Marathi Literature & Identity

The Maratha period consolidated Marathi as a literary and administrative language. The saint-poets — Tukārām, Rāmdās — created a devotional literature that defined Maratha cultural identity. Shivaji’s administration used Marathi for official correspondence, breaking Persian’s monopoly.

Fort Architecture

Shivaji built and captured over 300 hill forts across the Western Ghats — Raigad, Pratapgad, Sinhagad, Rajgad. These forts were the backbone of Maratha defense and remain iconic monuments of Maharashtra. The naval forts (jal-durg) at Sindhudurg and Vijaydurg protected the Maratha coast.

Administrative Innovation

The Maratha state combined Hindu political traditions with pragmatic innovation. The ashtapradhan (council of eight ministers) provided collective governance; the chauth and sardeshmukhi taxes funded the military; and the Peshwa’s office developed sophisticated diplomatic and intelligence networks.

VI

A Note on Method

The Maratha Confederacy lasted roughly 144 years (1674–1818 CE). This map shows the confederacy at approximately its peak influence (c. 1760 CE), just before the disaster at Panipat. The four great houses are shown at their approximate extents.

The confederacy was never a unitary state — the great houses operated with considerable independence, and actual control varied enormously from directly administered territory to areas where only tribute (chauth) was collected.
