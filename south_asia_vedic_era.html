The Later Vedic Age — Janapadas of the Gangetic Plain

The kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 1100 – c. 600 BCE — the age of the Kuru, Pañchāla, and eastward expansion of Vedic culture
I

The Later Vedic Age

In the centuries after the Ṛgvedic period, Vedic culture expanded eastward from the Sarasvatī–Dṛṣadvatī heartland into the upper and middle Gangetic plain. The Kuru and Pañchāla kingdoms emerged as the dominant powers — it was in their courts that the later Vedic texts (the Brāhmaṇas, Āraṇyakas, and early Upaniṣads) were composed, Vedic ritual was systematised, and the social order of varṇāśrama was codified.

Further east, the kingdoms of Kāśī, Kosala, and Videha represented the frontier of expanding Aryan culture — Videha's king Janaka is celebrated in the Upaniṣads as a philosopher-monarch. This was also the era of the great Painted Grey Ware (PGW) archaeological culture, whose distribution maps the spread of Vedic settlements.

The map below shows these early janapadas in their approximate locations — a world still defined by the Gangetic plain and the Indo-Gangetic Divide, before the urban revolution of the mahājanapada period.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Kuru Kingdom

Indraprastha / Hastināpura · c. 1200–600 BCE

The Kuru kingdom was the political and ritual centre of the Later Vedic world. The Kuru–Pañchāla alliance is regarded as the normative society of the Brāhmaṇa texts. It was in their courts that Vedic ritual was codified, the four-fold varṇa system crystallised, and the philosophical inquiries that led to the Upaniṣads began. The Mahābhārata war — whatever its historical basis — is set in this region.

Pañchāla

Ahicchatra · c. 1100–600 BCE

Paired with Kuru as the twin centre of Vedic orthodoxy. The Śatapatha Brāhmaṇa and other later Vedic texts were composed in this cultural milieu. By the sixth century BCE, Pañchāla would decline as Magadha and Kosala rose to prominence.

Videha (Mithilā)

Mithilā · ancient

King Janaka of Videha is a celebrated philosopher-king in the Upaniṣadic literature, hosting the sage Yājñavalkya and debating metaphysics. The Bṛhadāraṇyaka Upaniṣad preserves these dialogues — some of the most profound philosophical texts in any ancient literature.

Kāśī

Vārāṇasī · ancient

The ancient sacred city on the Ganges, already revered in the later Vedic period. Kāśī would become a centre of Buddhist teaching (the Buddha's first sermon was at Sarnath nearby) and remains one of the holiest sites in Hinduism.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

The Indo-Iranian Frontier

The northwestern regions maintained connections with the Iranian world. Gandhāra shared linguistic and cultural features with eastern Iran. By the late Vedic period, the Achaemenid Empire was expanding toward the Indus — a contact that would bring Persian administrative models and Aramaic script to the subcontinent.

The Dravidian South

Beyond the Vindhya range, southern India developed independently. The megalithic cultures of the Deccan and South India — known from iron-using burial traditions — were contemporary with the Later Vedic North but followed a distinct trajectory that would eventually produce the Tamil Sangam civilisation.

The Eastward Expansion

The most significant development of this era was the progressive movement of Aryan culture eastward along the Gangetic plain. Forest clearance (aided by iron tools), rice cultivation, and the establishment of new settlements transformed the eastern Gangetic plain from a frontier zone into the most productive agricultural region of the subcontinent.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

The Upaniṣads

The early Upaniṣads — Bṛhadāraṇyaka, Chāndogya, Aitareya — were composed in this milieu. They represent a philosophical revolution: moving from ritual to metaphysics, from sacrifice to knowledge (jñāna), from external action to internal realisation. The concepts of ātman, brahman, and karma were articulated here.

Vedic Ritual Codification

The Brāhmaṇa texts codified the elaborate Vedic ritual system — the agnicayana (fire-altar construction), the aśvamedha (horse sacrifice), the rājasūya (royal consecration). These texts are our primary source for understanding later Vedic society, economy, and political thought.

Painted Grey Ware

The Painted Grey Ware (PGW) ceramic tradition is the archaeological signature of this period. Found at sites across the western Gangetic plain — Hastināpura, Ahicchatra, Mathurā, Kauśāmbī — it marks the settlements of the Later Vedic peoples and provides material confirmation of the textual geography.

VI

A Note on Method

The Later Vedic period is known primarily from textual sources (the Brāhmaṇas, Āraṇyakas, and early Upaniṣads) correlated with the Painted Grey Ware archaeological horizon. No inscriptions exist from this era; the map is reconstructed from literary references and archaeological distribution patterns.

All territorial markings are highly schematic. These were not territorial states with fixed boundaries but kinship-based polities with shifting influence zones. The map should be read as showing approximate cultural affiliation rather than political control.
