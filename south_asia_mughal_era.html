The Mughal Empire — Akbar to Aurangzeb

The kingdoms of the subcontinent, c. 1526 – c. 1707 CE — the Pax Mughalica, the Rajput alliance, and the Deccan wars
I

The Mughal Empire

The Mughal Empire was the greatest state the Indian subcontinent had seen since the Mauryas. Founded by Babur in 1526 CE after his victory at Panipat, the empire reached its zenith under Akbar (r. 1556–1605), Shah Jahan (r. 1628–1658), and Aurangzeb (r. 1658–1707), at its peak controlling nearly the entire subcontinent from Kabul to the Kaveri.

Akbar’s genius lay not only in military conquest but in administrative integration: his mansabdari system, Rajput alliances, abolition of the jizya, and patronage of a syncretic courtly culture created a stable multi-ethnic, multi-religious empire. The Mughal court produced some of humanity’s greatest architecture — the Taj Mahal, Red Fort, Fatehpur Sikri — and attracted artists, poets, and scholars from across the Islamic world.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Akbar the Great

Agra / Fatehpur Sikri · r. 1556–1605 CE

Akbar transformed the Mughal state from a military conquest into a stable multi-ethnic empire. His mansabdari system integrated Rajput, Afghan, and Persian nobles; his abolition of the jizya and experiments with Dīn-i-Ilāhī signalled religious tolerance; and his Fatehpur Sikri was a masterpiece of syncretic architecture. By 1605, the empire stretched from Kabul to Bengal and from Kashmir to the Deccan frontier.

Shah Jahan

Agra / Delhi · r. 1628–1658 CE

The Taj Mahal (1632–1653) — built as a tomb for his wife Mumtaz Mahal — is the supreme monument of Mughal architecture and arguably the most beautiful building in the world. Shah Jahan also built the Red Fort and Jama Masjid in Delhi, and the Shalimar Gardens in Lahore. His reign was the Mughal golden age of art and architecture.

Aurangzeb

Delhi · r. 1658–1707 CE

Aurangzeb expanded the empire to its maximum territorial extent by conquering the Deccan Sultanates (Bijapur 1686, Golconda 1687), but his 27-year Deccan campaign drained the treasury. His reimposition of the jizya and destruction of Hindu temples alienated many subjects. The rising Maratha power of Shivaji and his successors proved an insoluble problem. Aurangzeb’s death in 1707 effectively ended Mughal greatness.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

The Maratha Challenge

Shivaji (r. 1674–1680) created an independent Maratha kingdom carved from Mughal and Bijapur territories. Despite Aurangzeb’s decades-long Deccan campaign, the Marathas could never be subdued — and after 1707, they would become the dominant power of the subcontinent.

European Trading Companies

During the Mughal period, European trading companies — Portuguese, Dutch, English, French — established coastal factories with Mughal permission. The English East India Company received its Surat farman in 1612 and the crucial Bengal farman in 1717 — the seeds of British India.

Central Asian & Persian Relations

The Mughals maintained complex relations with the Safavid Empire of Persia and the Central Asian Uzbek khanates. Kandahar changed hands repeatedly between Mughal and Safavid control. Persian was the court language, and Persian-trained artists and poets enriched Mughal culture.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

Mughal Architecture

From Humayun’s Tomb to the Taj Mahal, Mughal architecture represents a synthesis of Persian, Central Asian, and Indian traditions unmatched in the Islamic world. The use of white marble, pietra dura inlay, charbagh gardens, and monumental scale created a distinctive imperial style that influenced building across the subcontinent.

Mughal Painting

The Mughal painting tradition — initiated under Akbar by Persian masters and Indian artists — produced some of the finest miniature painting in world history. From the historical illustrations of the Akbarnāma to the naturalistic portraits and animal studies of Jahangir’s court, Mughal art achieved a unique synthesis of Persian refinement and Indian naturalism.

Administrative System

The mansabdari system assigned every noble a dual rank (personal rank and cavalry command), creating a meritocratic hierarchy. The empire was divided into subas (provinces), each with a governor, revenue collector, and judicial officer. Abu’l Fazl’s Ā’īn-i-Akbarī provides an extraordinarily detailed portrait of the system.

VI

A Note on Method

The Mughal Empire lasted 181 years from Babur to Aurangzeb’s death (1526–1707 CE); nominal Mughal emperors survived until 1857 but the empire was effectively defunct by 1720. This map shows the maximum extent under Aurangzeb (c. 1690 CE).

Even at its maximum, Mughal “control” varied enormously from directly administered provinces to barely tributary frontier zones. The coloured area represents the broadest reasonable interpretation of Mughal sovereignty.
