Cities of the Indus — the Harappan Civilisation

The subcontinent, c. 3300 – c. 1300 BCE — the earliest urban civilisation of South Asia, its cities, trade networks, and enigmatic script
I

The Indus Civilisation

The Indus Valley Civilisation — also called the Harappan Civilisation — was one of the three great cradles of urban life in the ancient world, alongside Mesopotamia and Egypt. At its peak (c. 2600–1900 BCE), it covered an area larger than either of its contemporaries, stretching from Shortughai in northern Afghanistan to Daimabad in Maharashtra, and from the Makran coast to the upper Gangetic plain.

Its great cities — Harappā, Mohenjo-daro, Dholāvīra, Rākhīgaṛhī, Lothal — display a remarkable uniformity of urban planning: grid-pattern streets, sophisticated drainage systems, standardised weights and measures, and a still-undeciphered script found on thousands of seal impressions. The civilisation engaged in long-distance trade with Mesopotamia, and its merchants' seals have been found at Ur and in the Persian Gulf.

The map below treats this as a city-and-trade plate rather than a territorial empire — showing the major urban centres, the geographic extent of the civilisation, and its trade connections with the wider ancient world.

III

The Kingdoms in Detail

Mohenjo-daro

Sindh · c. 2600–1900 BCE

The largest city of the Indus civilisation, with an estimated population of 30,000–50,000. Its Great Bath, granary, and citadel mound demonstrate sophisticated urban planning. The city had a covered drainage system unmatched in the ancient world until the Roman Empire.

Harappā

Punjab · c. 3300–1300 BCE

The type-site of the civilisation, discovered in the 1820s and excavated from the 1920s. Harappā's multiple mounds represent different functional quarters — residential, industrial, and ceremonial. Its cemetery provides key evidence for Harappan burial practices and social stratification.

Dholāvīra

Kutch (Gujarat) · c. 2650–1450 BCE

A city of remarkable water engineering — over sixteen reservoirs capturing seasonal monsoon runoff in the arid Rann of Kutch. Its three-part division (citadel, middle town, lower town) and monumental gateways are unique. A large inscription in the undeciphered Indus script was found above one gateway.

Rākhīgaṛhī

Haryana · c. 3300–1300 BCE

Now recognised as the largest Harappan site by area, Rākhīgaṛhī lies in the Sarasvatī–Ghaggar basin. Recent DNA studies from its cemetery have provided crucial genetic data about the Harappan population.

Lothal

Gujarat · c. 2400–1600 BCE

A port city with what its excavator identified as a dockyard — the oldest in the world if the identification is correct. Lothal was a centre of bead manufacture and maritime trade, linking the Harappan world to the Persian Gulf and Mesopotamia.

IV

Powers Beyond the Frontier

Mesopotamia & the Gulf Trade

Indus seals and goods have been found at Ur, Kish, and Tell Asmar in Mesopotamia. Cuneiform texts mention Meluhha — almost certainly the Indus region — as a source of carnelian, lapis lazuli, ivory, and timber. The trade was conducted via the Persian Gulf, with Dilmun (Bahrain) as an intermediary.

The Oxus Civilisation

The Bactria-Margiana Archaeological Complex (BMAC) in Central Asia was contemporary with the Mature Harappan phase. Evidence of contact — shared artefact types, Indus seals at Shortughai — suggests that the Indus civilisation engaged with Central Asian networks as well as the Mesopotamian ones.

The Peninsular South

South of the Harappan zone, chalcolithic and neolithic cultures existed independently. The Southern Neolithic (Ash Mound tradition) and the Deccan chalcolithic cultures developed their own agricultural systems and settlement patterns, interacting with the Harappan periphery but not part of the urban system.

V

The Cultural Efflorescence

The Indus Script

The Indus script — found on thousands of seals, pottery, and copper tablets — remains undeciphered. With an average text length of only 5 signs, it may represent a logographic or logo-syllabic system. Without decipherment, the political structure, religious beliefs, and even the language(s) of the Harappan people remain matters of scholarly debate.

Urban Planning & Hydraulics

Harappan cities display a degree of urban planning unmatched in their era: grid-pattern streets, standardised brick sizes (4:2:1 ratio), covered drainage systems, public wells, and (at Mohenjo-daro) a Great Bath that may have had ritual significance. Water management — from Dholāvīra's reservoirs to Lothal's dock — was a defining achievement.

Craft & Technology

Harappan artisans produced exquisite craft goods: carnelian beads (drilled with a technique unique to the Indus), shell bangles, faience ornaments, steatite seals with animal motifs, and bronze figurines. The Dancing Girl of Mohenjo-daro and the Priest-King sculpture are iconic works of early world art.

VI

A Note on Method

This plate departs from the territorial-empire format used for later periods. The Indus Civilisation was not a single state (as far as we know) but a cultural zone characterised by shared material culture, urban planning conventions, weights and measures, and the undeciphered script. The map shows the geographic extent of Harappan-type sites and the major urban centres within them.

Dates follow the standard periodisation: Early Harappan (c. 3300–2600 BCE), Mature Harappan (c. 2600–1900 BCE), Late Harappan (c. 1900–1300 BCE). The "decline" of the civilisation was not a catastrophic collapse but a gradual de-urbanisation and fragmentation.

Sources & Further Reading

Primary Evidence

  • Archaeological excavations at Mohenjo-daro, Harappā, Dholāvīra, Rākhīgaṛhī, Lothal.
  • Indus seal corpus — over 4,000 inscribed objects.
  • Mesopotamian cuneiform references to Meluhha.
  • Indus-type artefacts found at Ur, Bahrain, and Shortughai.
